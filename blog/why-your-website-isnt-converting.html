Why Your Website Isn't Converting

April 22, 2026 · Kevin Patrick · 6 min

Five Seconds. That's It.

Pull up your homepage on your phone right now. Set a five-second timer. Show it to a friend who doesn't know what your company does. When the timer buzzes, close the page and ask: "What do we do? Who do we do it for? And what would you do next if you were a customer?"

If your friend stumbles, I'll save you the scroll. Your website isn't converting because it's not trying to.

I've audited hundreds of sites across the startups and established businesses I've worked with over the past decade. The bad news: 80% of them fail the five-second test. The good news: the fix is almost never "rebuild it from scratch." It's usually three changes that take less than a week.

Most Websites Are Brochures, Not Conversations

Here's the mistake that quietly kills conversion for most early-stage companies. You treat your website like a brochure — a static document describing what your company is. You list features. You talk about your team. You have a "Services" page with nine services and a "Contact" page that sits at the end like a tip jar.

A brochure is a one-way broadcast. A sales conversation is a two-way exchange. Your best salespeople don't open a meeting with a 12-slide company overview. They open by asking about the customer's problem and listening. Your website needs to do the same thing — and most don't.

The visitor lands on your page with one question burning: Can you solve my specific problem? The job of your homepage is to answer that question in under five seconds — and then offer one obvious next step to keep the conversation going.

The Three Things That Actually Convert

After building and rebuilding sites for early-stage companies through Trinity Forge, the pattern is painfully consistent. The sites that convert well — 4-7% of visitors into leads, versus the typical 0.5-1.5% — do three things:

1. Answer the #1 question in five seconds. Not your mission. Not your history. The one sentence that tells the visitor: here's what we do, here's who it's for, here's why it matters. Think of it as the elevator pitch your best salesperson would give if they had one breath.

A contractor's homepage shouldn't open with "Quality craftsmanship since 2014." It should open with "We renovate Atlanta homes for families who want the work done right the first time — without losing their kitchen for three months."

2. Offer one clear next step. Pick one. "Book a free 20-minute consultation." Or "Get a quote in 48 hours." Or "Start your free trial." One. Not eight navigation items, a newsletter signup, a chat widget, and a phone number buried in the footer.

Every additional option cuts conversion roughly in half. That's not a rule of thumb I made up — it's the consistent pattern across 120+ site rebuilds I've either led or been brought in to fix.

3. Zero friction to start the conversation. If your "Contact Us" form asks for company size, industry, budget range, project timeline, and a message, you've designed a filter — not a funnel. The right form on a first conversation has three fields: name, email, one sentence about what you need. That's it. You can ask the other questions after you're on a call with them.

The Hidden Conversion Killers

Beyond the three essentials, four specific things silently sabotage conversion on startup sites:

→ Slow load time. Every additional second of load time above 2 seconds drops conversion by roughly 7%. If your site takes 5 seconds to paint, you've lost 21% of visitors before they saw anything.

→ Stock photography that screams "I bought this template." Nothing tells a visitor "we're not real yet" like a smiling headset woman on your homepage.

→ No proof. One testimonial. One case study. One number you've delivered. If your site has zero evidence that you've done the work, visitors assume you haven't.

→ Mobile that's actually just desktop squeezed smaller. 67% of small-business visitors are on their phones. If your site requires zooming, you've lost them.

A Real Example from a Forge Sprint

A field-services founder came to Forge last year with a site built by a $4,800 agency engagement. Beautiful animations. 11-page architecture. 47-second average load time on mobile. Lead conversion: 0.4%.

We rebuilt it in a 3-week Forge sprint. Single-page site. One value statement above the fold: "We replace commercial HVAC systems for Atlanta buildings in under 48 hours — or the next service call is free." One CTA: "Get a 15-minute estimate." Three-field form. Mobile load time: 1.2 seconds.

Four months later, conversion was 5.8%. Same traffic. 14.5x the leads. The founder's words: "I had a website. I didn't have a selling website."

Rebuild or Refine?

If your site fails the five-second test and you're getting less than 1.5% conversion, you don't need a refresh. You need a rebuild — and the economics of Forge's AI-powered launch sprints mean a proper rebuild is now 2-4 weeks and $3K-$10K, not 3 months and $40K.

If your site passes the five-second test but you're still under-converting, the fix is usually simpler: tighten the headline, cut the navigation to three items, simplify the form, and put a real testimonial above the fold. I can usually diagnose it in a 20-minute call.

Your website is the only salesperson that works 24/7, never calls in sick, and talks to every single person who ever types your name into Google. If you've been tolerating one that's been underperforming for a year, you're not saving money. You're losing every lead that bounced before they ever got to a conversation.

One more pattern worth naming: I've never seen a founder rebuild a broken website and regret it. I've met plenty who regretted waiting. The typical story is some version of "I kept patching it for 14 months before I finally redid it properly, and the first 30 days after relaunch produced more leads than the entire previous year." If your gut has been telling you the site isn't working, your gut is right. The only question is how many more months of bounced traffic you're willing to pay for before you act.

Five seconds. One question. One next step. That's the bar.
